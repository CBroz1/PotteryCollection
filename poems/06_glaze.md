---
title: "Glaze"
---

Suspended minerals in water —
the glaze looks nothing
like itself before firing.
This matte tan will turn deep blue.
This milky slip will copper.

We measure by recipe,
by memory, by hunch,
mixing buckets that smell
like iron and feldspar,
brushing them on thick
where we want the color to pool
in the grooves and feet.

There is a glaze called ash
made from wood fire,
from what the flame left behind —
trees translated
into the blue-green skin of bowls.

I use a celadon on my tea cups,
the faintest possible green,
the color of shallow water
over sand. My grandmother
had cups this color.

She held hers with both hands
the way people hold
what they don't want to spill,
what they don't want to lose —
though of course
she lost it anyway.
Everything does.
